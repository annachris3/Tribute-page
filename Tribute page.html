Jan Kaplicky

The Neofuturistic Architect

Kaplicky was born on 18 April 1937 in Prague, Czechoslovakia. Between 1956 and 1962 he studied at the College of Applied Arts and Architecture and Design (VSUP) in Prague, receiving a Diploma in Architecture. He worked in private practice in Czechoslovakia between 1964 and 1968. In the wake of the Prague Spring, the Soviet invasion of Czechoslovakia, he escaped to London in September 1968 with fellow architect Jaroslav Vokoun carrying only US$100 and a few pairs of socks.

Here's a timeline of his career:

  • 1969 - In England, Kaplicky first worked for Denys Lasdun and Partners, where he worked on DRU extension at Aybrook Street, London and later helping to develop the design for the Centre Georges Pompidou (constructed 1971–1977) in Paris.
  • 1974 - He joined Foster Associates, now Foster and Partners.
  • 1979 - Jan Kapliscky set up his own architectural think tank called Future Systems with David Nixon, and began to develop an architectural style that combined organic forms with high tech futurism.
  • 1980s - His design for the Grand Buildings in Trafalgar Square, London, was a free-form monocoque structure pierced by portholes; it lost to a more conventional reconstruction of an Edwardian facade.
  • 1991 - Kaplicky was a finalist for the Prince Philip Prize for designer of the year in 1991, for the first 15 years of its existence Future Systems received few commissions.
  • 1999 - Won the Royal Institute of British Architects (RIBA) Stirling Prize – considered the most prestigious architecture award in the UK, for the building of the new media centre at Lord's Cricket Ground in London.
  • 2000 - He was made an Honourable Fellow of RIBA.Future Systems' next major project, the iconic Selfridges Building in the Bull Ring in Birmingham, won six awards.
  • 2004 - The Selfridges Building won the RIBA Award for Architecture.
  • 2007 - Kaplicky won the design competition for the new Czech National Library building.
  • 2009 - He collapsed on a street in Prague with heart failure and could not be revived by emergency services.

"I also don't think you need to go anywhere particular to be creative; people just use that as an excuse. But I do think a lot of creativity depends on your relationships with other people, your personal relationships, your partner or whatever. Your personal happiness or unhappiness comes out in your work, it's a reflection of your emotional state and you can't separate the two."
Jan Kaplicky (1937-2009)
